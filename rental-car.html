Vehicle deep dive · verified 2026-05-16

Opel Mokka 2021+ (second-gen "Mokka B") — is it actually a good car?

One-line verdict: Solid choice for THIS trip — composed on Alpine roads, right size, decent reliability for a 2-year-old rental, fuel-efficient on the 1.2 turbo. Two things to know: (1) the fuel-pipe recall window (May 2022 → Mar 2023) directly overlaps current rental-fleet build dates — ASK at the counter whether the VIN was in the recall and remediated; (2) the PureTech 1.2 engine has known cold-start hiccups and oil-consumption complaints past 20-30k km — rental examples will be high-mileage. Not bad enough to swap the recommendation. The Mokka stays as #1.

Reliability — mixed but not alarming

  • What Car? used review (2021-present): Mokka B sits mid-pack in the small-SUV reliability survey — electrical, engine, and gearbox flagged as the soft spots. Not "lemon" territory but not Toyota-bulletproof either. Source: What Car? reliability page.
  • The 1.2 PureTech turbo engine (the one in the rental): common faults appearing as early as 20,000 km — loss of power, rough idling, increased oil consumption, faulty ignition coils, jerky acceleration at low revs, and unreliable cold-starts below 5°C. Mostly nuisance-level, not breakdown-level, but worth knowing the morning of pickup. Source: AutoDoc Mokka B fault catalogue.
  • Brake wear: pads need replacing at 25-35k km (above-average wear). Irrelevant for a 7-day rental but a tell that the platform is heavier on consumables than competitors. Source: AutoDoc.
  • Infotainment quirks: Bluetooth + Android Auto random restarts and no-media-audio bugs reported on owners' forums. Pre-test phone pairing in the airport lot before driving off so any failure surfaces at the counter, not 90 min later on the A1. Source: Mokka owners forum.

Active recall — ASK BEFORE PICKUP

Fuel-pipe recall (Stellantis, 2024): Mokka units built between 9 May 2022 and 22 March 2023 may have a loose nut on the high-pressure fuel pipe, with 11 confirmed engine fires in France triggering the recall. ~72,000 vehicles affected across Mokka / Corsa / Astra / Grandland / Frontera + Peugeot 208 / 2008 + Citroën C3 + Fiat 600 + Jeep Avenger. Remediation is a dealer-level pipe-replacement / nut-retorque — fast and free. Most rental fleets have completed the campaign, but ask at the counter: "Has this VIN been through the fuel-pipe recall?" Get the answer in writing. If the agent can't confirm or the car is in the build-date window with no recall paperwork, decline that unit and ask for a swap. Source: car-recalls.eu · Source: Auto Express recall coverage.

Safety — Euro NCAP 4★ (2021 test)

  • Four stars, not five. The Mokka B fails to clear the 5★ threshold because it lacks AEB for cyclists and lacks a centre airbag (the between-front-seats one that prevents head clash in side impact). Source: NCAP coverage.
  • Standard safety kit: AEB (car + pedestrian), lane-keep, speed-limit recognition, driver-attention monitor. Good enough for two adults on Austrian motorways at 130 km/h. Not a deal-breaker, but the VW Golf alternate IS 5★ — that's what the €430 premium buys. Source: Euro NCAP ratings explorer.

Alpine driving — composed, not powerful (but enough)

  • 1.2 turbo (130 hp): 0-100 km/h in ~9.2s. Plenty for two adults climbing the B305 toward Berchtesgaden or the Postalmstrasse switchbacks. Not a fast car, but the turbo gives torque where you need it on grades. Source: Cars.co.za 2022 review.
  • Handling: reviewers consistently note "no body roll despite crossover height" + "precise, fleet-footed cornering." The higher seating position vs the Golf is the real Alpine win — easier to spot apex on hairpins. Source: AutoTrader long review.
  • Front-wheel drive only at rental level. No AWD option in standard European rental fleets. For paved Alpine roads (Großglockner, Rossfeldstrasse, the standard Salzkammergut loop) this is fine — Austrian + Bavarian mountain roads are engineered for FWD passenger cars, and you're driving in July with zero ice exposure. If you decide to take a forest-service gravel road, that's the Suzuki Vitara's job, not the Mokka's.

Comfort + cargo — front good, back tight, boot tight

  • Front seats: 6-way adjustable, well-bolstered, comfortable on multi-hour drives per Carbuyer + AutoExpress. The driving position is the Mokka's strongest practical feature. Source: AutoExpress practicality.
  • Suspension is firm — composed in corners but transmits more of rough surfaces than the Peugeot 2008 cousin. On smooth Austrian autobahn it's a non-issue; on the older B-road sections between Hallstatt and Bad Aussee you'll feel expansion joints. Source: Carbuyer practicality.
  • Rear seat space is tight for adults. Irrelevant for this trip — two adults in the front. Listed for completeness.
  • 350L boot, REAL test: fits ~3 large suitcases (70L each) per published luggage tests. For this trip's specific load — 2 medium suitcases + the kitchen kit + a daily groceries bag — the answer is "comfortably yes, with a parcel shelf you can remove for the extra-loaded day." No headroom for one of you to also bring a duffel. Source: Mokka boot test.

Fuel economy — real numbers, not brochure

  • 1.2 turbo gasoline: WLTP brochure 6.1 L/100km; real-world owner data ~5.8 L/100km on mixed driving per motoreu data, ~7.0-7.5 L/100km on mountain-heavy days. For a ~600 km week that's ~35-45 L of gasoline = ~€60-75 at European July 2026 pump prices. Budget €80 for the trip's fuel line to be safe. Source: motoreu real-world fuel data.
  • Mild-hybrid 48V variant: 4.9 L/100km WLTP — a real ~20% saving vs the pure-gasoline. Over 600 km the difference is ~7 L = ~€12-14. That's the basis for the "pick hybrid for the principle, not the math" line on the Mokka Hybrid card. Source: Stellantis hybrid press release.

Sixt publishes a clear cross-border schedule (verified from their AT terms page): Germany = Zone I, €11.90/day, capped €39.95 for the week (~₪158). Pre-authorisable at booking. This is the cleanest cross-border policy on the page.

Hertz, Avis, Europcar, Enterprise: all four typically permit Germany on Salzburg-airport rentals by default (the German border is 2 km from SZG). Their websites do not publish a consolidated cross-border schedule like Sixt does — you ask the counter or read the contract fine-print before driving off.

Aggregator listings (DiscoverCars, Kayak, EconomyBookings, Wisecars, Sicily By Car, Dryyve, WheeGo, Buchbinder, Megadrive, Alamo at SZG): cross-border policies are set by the underlying supplier, not the aggregator. The aggregator shows you the price but the rules don't surface until you read the fine-print PDF at booking OR ask at the counter. Treat every aggregator listing as "DE likely yes, confirm at counter." Get Germany authorisation in writing on the contract before driving toward Berchtesgaden.

What I could NOT verify: exact 2026 Hertz / Avis / Europcar Germany cross-border fees from their public websites — these vary by car class and need a live booking quote or a counter conversation. The German-border default is industry practice for SZG rentals but not a written guarantee — get it on the contract.

The caveats — read these before booking (vignette, IDP, fuel, hours)

Vignette (Austrian motorway toll sticker)

Austrian motorways require a vignette — ~€11.50 for a 10-day sticker (2026 rate). Most rentals out of Salzburg airport include a digital vignette pre-affixed, but it's not automatic — confirm at the counter. If yours doesn't include it, buy a 10-day digital vignette from asfinag.at before driving onto the A1 / A10. Note: the stretch from the German border at Walserberg to Salzburg-Nord exit is vignette-exempt — the airport-to-old-town leg itself is free, but everything beyond Salzburg-Nord requires it (Hallstatt, Werfen, etc.).

Grossglockner High Alpine Road — separate toll (~€42 / 2026)

The Grossglockner Hochalpenstrasse is a private toll road, not on the vignette. Day ticket ~€42 per car as of 2026 (verify at grossglockner.at). Not in the current itinerary — but if you swap a day for it, that's a separate cash line.

International Driving Permit (IDP)

Allison drives on an Israeli license. Austria + Germany both technically recommend an IDP alongside the home-country license for rentals. The IDP is free in Israel via MEMSI — pick it up before the trip. Without it, most counters will rent anyway but it removes a refusal risk on arrival.

Fuel policy

Ask for full-to-full (return with a full tank). The full-to-empty option looks cheaper on paper but the per-litre markup is brutal. Last gas station before SZG on the way back is on the B1 / A1 ring road, ~3 min before the rental return.

Pickup hours Friday morning

Salzburg airport rental desks open 05:30-06:00 most days; flight lands 08:00, so the counter is open by then. Sixt + Hertz + Europcar + Avis + Enterprise all in-terminal — no shuttle. DiscoverCars-listed brokers (Megadrive, Buchbinder, Goldcar, Sicily By Car) are mostly shuttle pickups — add 10-15 minutes from terminal to keys.

Drop-off Friday morning at 05:00

5am drop-off for the early flight — confirm the return lot has 24-hour access (most do at SZG; you leave the keys in a drop-box). Pre-fill a return-receipt photo: take a time-stamped phone photo of the fuel gauge + odometer + a quick walk-around at drop-off so any phantom-damage claims later can be disputed.
