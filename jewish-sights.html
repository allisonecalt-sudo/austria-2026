Jewish Salzburg — places worth a stop

All optional — none locked. Judengasse + Stolpersteine + synagogue courtyard slot in at zero detour (on the Chabad walk). Aigen cemetery is a deliberate 90-min visit (book via IKG). Mauthausen is a full heavy day — replaces a lake day if yes.

A shared-interest section. None of these are in the locked itinerary — not because of interest mismatch, but because of time and weight. Two are walking-distance from the apartment so they'd slot in at zero opportunity cost (Judengasse, Stolpersteine). The synagogue courtyard is a 15-min stop. Mauthausen is a separate full day and emotionally heavy — surfaced honestly so it's a real shared decision, not a surprise.

Emotional-weight key: 🌿 light history — context, plaques, medieval streetscape. ⚖️ medium — active community sites, accessible memorial objects. ⚫ heavy memorial — Holocaust commemoration as the primary content.

In the Altstadt — zero detour, on the walk between apartment and Chabad

Walking-distance Jewish history

🌿 Judengasse — the medieval Jewish quarter

What: A narrow shopping lane curving between Alter Markt and Waagplatz, the center of Salzburg's Jewish community from the 12th century until the expulsion of 1498. The synagogue stood at Judengasse 15 (per a 1370 record) — today a hotel. The historical plaques along the lane tell the story.

Why it's interesting: Documents from the 12th century report a Jewish quarter here — predating most surviving Ashkenazi infrastructure in central Europe. Burnings in 1349 (Black-Death blood libel) and 1404; final expulsion 1498. Then no Jewish community in Salzburg again until the 19th century.

Time: 20-30 min slow stroll if you read the plaques. Walk: ~9 min from master Linzergasse to Alter Markt, then the Judengasse curves out from there. Cost: Free.

⚖️ Stolpersteine route in the Altstadt + Andräviertel

What: Brass paving stones embedded in the sidewalk in front of the last freely-chosen residences of Jews and other victims deported under Nazi rule. Each stone names a person, dates of birth, deportation, and death. Salzburg has over 519 stones as of 2025 — and the very first one in Salzburg was laid on Linzergasse itself in August 2007 (Rupertgasse 4 area is dense with them; multiple clusters along Linzergasse).

Why it's interesting: The chibur form of this — public, daily, unavoidable, no museum required, no entrance fee. You'll cross several without looking just walking apartment to Chabad to Spar. The interactive map lets you build a personalized route.

Time: 30-90 min for a deliberate route. Walk: on the apartment-to-Chabad-to-old-town path already. Cost: Free.

stolpersteine-salzburg.at — interactive map →

⚖️ Salzburg Synagogue (IKG) + Kristallnacht memorial

What: The active synagogue at Lasserstrasse 8, built originally 1901 by Bohemian manufacturer Ignaz Glaser. Destroyed shortly after the 1938 Nazi annexation; rebuilt 1967-68. The reconstructed sanctuary has dove-gray walls, prewar Torahs, vintage menorahs around the bimah, and a Kristallnacht memorial sculpture in the courtyard. ~100 Jews live in Salzburg today.

Why it's interesting: The active building of a community that almost wasn't — 250 Jews in 1938 → essentially zero by 1945 → re-established in 1953. The courtyard memorial is open and visible without an interior visit. The building is already on the Shabbat page as the backup shul; this is a context add.

Time: 15 min courtyard visit; longer if office is open and they show interior. Walk: 10-12 min from master Linzergasse. Cost: Free; for an interior visit call ahead +43 662 87 2228.

Across town — needs a deliberate visit

Jewish cemetery, Aigen

⚖️ Salzburg Jewish Cemetery (Israelitischer Friedhof Aigen)

What: The Jewish cemetery at Uferstrasse 47 in the Aigen district, southeast of the old town. Established 1893. About 450 graves. Closed for security reasons by default — visits go through the IKG office. They offer guided tours several times a year, during which Jewish death and mourning rituals are discussed.

Why it's interesting: Pre-war community visible as named graves — the inverse of the Stolpersteine. One source lists visiting hours as 10am-4pm Sun-Fri but verify with IKG office before going — security closures are real.

Time: 60-90 min with travel. Distance from old town: 4 km — 15 min by car, S-Bahn line to Hallein stops in Aigen. Cost: Free. Required: contact IKG office in advance.

Outside Salzburg — a real day-trip, heavy

Mauthausen Memorial

⚫ KZ-Gedenkstätte Mauthausen (concentration-camp memorial)

What: The main Mauthausen camp memorial, on the Danube about 20 km east of Linz. One of the largest WWII concentration-camp memorials in Austria. Operated 1938-1945 with sub-camps including Ebensee + Gusen + St. Georgen. Now a state memorial site with preserved buildings, the quarry, the "Stairs of Death," monuments by each country whose citizens died there, and an extensive documentation center.

Hours (verified 2026): Mar 1 – Oct 26 daily 09:00-17:30 (last entry 16:45). Admission: FREE — no registration required. Audio guide in 12 languages via free app (download before going — no full coverage on site).

The honest weight tag: This is a full memorial visit, not a museum. People come out wrung out. A full self-guided walk-through is 3-4 hours minimum on site. Combined with the drive it's a whole day — and an emotionally specific day. Read into "Survival in Auschwitz" (Primo Levi) territory; it's not a casual stop. Both of you decide together whether the day is right for it.

Distance from Salzburg: ~120-165 km (varies by route), 1h45m-2h drive each way. Not in the current itinerary; would replace one of the lake days. If it's a yes, the Thursday transit day from Hallstatt back toward Salzburg is the most natural slot (Hallstatt → Mauthausen ~1h40m → Salzburg ~1h45m). Cost: €40 fuel + tolls, no admission.

If we want a guide

Salzburg Jewish-heritage walking tours

Salzburg has private operators who do thematic Jewish-heritage routes. None of these are confirmed-bookable for our specific date without contacting them — but the infrastructure exists.

Salzburg Top Tours salzburg-toptours.com — certified guide Eugene; fully customizable private walking tours €200-350 for 2-3 hours.
Panorama Tours panoramatours.com — private themed walks with certified Austrian guides.
Context Travel contexttravel.com Salzburg — historian-led; ask specifically about Jewish-heritage focus.
ToursByLocals toursbylocals.com Salzburg — find a local guide and request a Jewish-history focus.

For a Jewish-heritage-specific guide, the cleanest path is probably emailing IKG Salzburg directly (office@ikg-salzburg.at) — they sometimes connect visitors with members or community-trained guides for a personal walk. Same email gets you the cemetery visit, so it's one ask.
