The idea that makes it work

Why it's “like milk”

חלב סתם

“We rely on milk because it's so watched.”

That's the whole backbone — and you named it. Chalav stam (Rav Moshe Feinstein): regular European milk is trusted not because a rabbi stood there, but because government regulation is so tight that non-kosher milk basically can't get in. Austria is EU, so the OU's own poskim apply it here.

Ingredient-based kashrut extends that logic to labels: EU law forces full, honest ingredient disclosure — so the label becomes the thing you trust, the way you trust the milk. Most Modern-Orthodox rely on chalav stam; a stricter minority uses only chalav yisrael — for them, uncertified dairy is out and plant-based is the answer.

Canned tuna — lenient 🟢
Three grounds together: steamed first · not oleh al shulchan melachim · made industrially.
"Industrial" isn't a heter on its own 🟡
"Some use it as a factor in leniency. If that is the only factor to be lenient it would be more difficult." → it supports other reasons; it doesn't stand alone. So don't assume "factory-made = fine."
"King's table" is subjective 🟡
"It is subjective and can change." → there's no clean test you can apply in the aisle. When it's the deciding factor, ask.
Chips & popcorn 🟢
"Ok according to most."
Canned vegetables 🟢
"Likely ok" — peas, corn, mushrooms, uncertified.
Cold-smoked salmon 🟢
"Ok" — never actually cooked.
Couscous / pre-cooked rice 🟡
"Not sure." Factory-cooked, you just add water — still open. Treat as undecided.
Part-cooked, you finish it 🟢 — the general rule
"If something is partially cooked and you finish the cooking it is ok from bishul akum."
→ This settles frozen fries and anything else the factory only part-cooks. Note the limit: it turns on you doing real cooking — so it does not cover instant couscous, which is fully steamed at the factory and only rehydrated.

Where your line sits

Cooked by them: bishul akum vs pas palter

🔎 Researched The framework below is sourced research. Gil's own answers are in What Gil said.

בישול עכו״ם · פת פלטר

You don't rely on bishul akum leniencies, but you do accept pas palter. Those aren't in conflict — bread is its own category with its own leniency.

Bishul akum only bites when BOTH are true: the food (1) can't be eaten raw, and (2) is important enough to serve at a real meal. Fail either test and the rule doesn't apply.

Canned tuna 🟢 נשאל ונענה
Asked Gil — there's room to be lenient, on three grounds: (1) it's steamed first, (2) the finished product is not oleh al shulchan melachim (canned tuna isn't "king's-table" food), and (3) it's made industrially. So tuna is back on the menu — still read the can (watch broth, Aroma, vegetable-broth additions).
Frozen fries 🟢 — settled
Gil: "If something is partially cooked and you finish the cooking it is ok from bishul akum." The factory par-fries; you finish it. And McCain Frites 1·2·3 is KLBD-certified at Interspar — so you can skip the question entirely.
Bread & baked goods 🟢
Bread, crispbread, crackers, pretzels — all pas, covered by pas palter. Fine for you.
Everything you cook 🟢
Pasta, rice, oats, legumes, frozen peas, fresh produce — you cook it, so bishul akum never starts. Plus edible-raw things (tomatoes, jam, juice, olives) and milk/yogurt/chocolate, which aren't "cooking" at all.
The structural point: because you're self-catering and cooking from raw, bishul akum mostly evaporates — it only touches what arrives already cooked. Your kitchen is the fix.

The method

How to read a label — in order

🔎 Researched Gil approved the method itself: "no difference between a long list and a short list if you understand the ingredients." This is the understanding part.

  1. 1
    Look for a hechsher first. A recognized symbol on the pack and you're done — no reading needed. This is the best outcome, not the fallback.
  2. 2
    Look for the V-Label vegan mark. 🌱 It's audited and covers additives, carriers, flavourings, enzymes and processing aids — so it settles the entire animal-ingredient question in one glance. It does not settle dairy equipment, grape, or cooked-by-them.
  3. 3
    Find the word Zutaten. That's the ingredient list — the only part of the package that matters. Everything else is marketing.
  4. 4
    Scan for the five red words. Any one = put it back.
    GelatineKarminKäse Wein · Weinessig · Traubensaft · Balsamico Schweineschmalz · tierisches Fett · tierisches Lab
    That's the whole hard-stop list. If none of those are there, you've cleared the big ones.
  5. 5
    Check if it's dairy. Milch · Molke · Milcheiweiß · Magermilchpulver · Butter · Sahne/Obers. All fine for you under chalav stam — you're just noting that it's dairy, so plan the meal around it.
  6. 6
    Now the judgment calls. Aroma · E471 / E472 · E570 · E572 · Glycerin. If step 2 found a V-Label, these are already resolved. If not — these are the open ones. (E570/E572/E491–495 are the weakest; those are worth putting back.)
  7. 7
    Ignore these — they are not kashrut information. "Kann Spuren von … enthalten" (an allergy warning) · Bio (a farming label) · lecithin, citric acid, pectin, plant colours. Stop spending attention on them.
  8. 8
    Last, ask what kind of food it is. Baked → pas palter, fine. Raw and you cook it → fine, bishul akum never starts. Arrives already cooked and ready to eat → that's the only category where the cooked-by-them question even applies.
Two tricks that actually help 🟢
① Named flavour beats bare flavour. "natürliches Erdbeeraroma" must legally be ≥95% from real strawberry. A bare "Aroma" names nothing and can lawfully include animal-origin material.
② The oil line is honest. Vegetable oils must name the plant ("Pflanzenöl (Raps)"), and animal fat must say tierisch. So a fat line that names only plants means there's no animal fat as an ingredient.
What the label will never tell you 🔴
The origin of an additive. EU law requires only the class and the E-number — never whether E471 came from a plant or an animal. Silence is the legal default. Stop looking for it; look for the V-Label instead.

The traps

What the label hides — and when to just put it back

🔎 Researched A clean ingredient list is not the same as a clean product. These are the things EU law lets a manufacturer leave OUT entirely — so on these items, reading harder doesn't help.

1. Rennet doesn't have to be declared
It's legally a processing aid, so it can be used and never appear. Seeing Lab proves it's there — not seeing it proves nothing. Worse: on some dairy products the ingredient list may be omitted altogether. This is exactly the skyr trap.
2. Additive origin is never declared
E471 and the whole fatty-acid family: EU law requires the class and the E-number, and nothing about plant vs animal. Silence is the legal default, not a clue.
3. "Aroma" can legally hide a lot
EU law explicitly permits flavourings made from animal material, and the carriers and solvents inside a flavouring may be left off the list entirely — typically glycerin, alcohol, propylene glycol or oil. Glycerin and alcohol are exactly the kosher-sensitive ones.
4. The allergen line misses almost everything you avoid
Allergen law forces milk and fish to surface even inside an Aroma — but there is no allergen entry for meat, gelatin, grape/wine, or carmine. Four of your five hard stops are invisible to it.
5. "Milch" in a flavouring ≠ milk powder
The declaration just says Milch — so a cheese-derived flavouring reads identically to a milk-powder one. You cannot tell them apart.
6. Animal fat needn't name the animal
"tierisches Fett" is a complete, legal declaration — pork, beef or a blend all look the same. (It does have to say "animal," which is why a plants-only oil line is trustworthy.)
So: put it back when the risk lives in the invisible part. Concretely — ① anything cheese-adjacent (skyr, quark/Topfen, cheese spreads), because rennet is invisible; ② anything whose flavour is meaty, cheesy, creamy or winey and carries Aroma; ③ anything where an emulsifier is doing structural work (it holds the product together) with no V-Label; ④ anything trading on "vegetarisch" — that permits dairy and rennet, unlike vegan. On those, no amount of careful reading gets you an answer — buy a certified version or choose something simpler.

Yogurt: the dairy one is cleaner than the plant one

You'd assume the soy yogurt is the safer bet. Compare the real ingredient lists and it's the other way round — the plain dairy one has one ingredient; the plant one has eleven.

🟢 Plain Naturjoghurt — zero open flags

Zutaten: Joghurt 1 % Fett, aus pasteurisierter Milch

That is the entire list. Run it through everything:

  • Milk → chalav stam ✓
  • Culture-set, not rennet → no cheese question at all (this is exactly what skyr fails)
  • No Aroma, no emulsifiers, no additives — none of the open questions even apply

Buy: nöm Joghurt löffelfest 1% 250 g (BILLA + SPAR) — or honestly any own-brand Naturjoghurt, which is the same single ingredient. nöm just has the paperwork (expired 2023, Rabbi A. Katz, marked chalav stam).

🟡 Alpro Soja Joghurt — check for the vegan mark

Fine in principle (plant, no milk), but two items land on the unresolved pile:

  • natürliches Aroma — the exact thing still open with Gil. EU law permits animal-origin flavourings, and carriers (glycerin, alcohol) can be legally omitted.
  • Fettsäureester der Ascorbinsäure — that's ascorbyl palmitate (E304), a fatty-acid ester. Same family as E471: origin never declared.

Both are settled the moment you see a V-Label / vegan mark — it's audited and explicitly covers flavourings, carriers and additives. Alpro is a vegan brand, so it almost certainly carries it. Glance at the tub.

Everything else on it is green: pectin · citric acid & sodium citrate · tocopherol extracts · Vitamin D2 (plant — D3 is the lanolin one) · cultures · "Kann Spuren von Schalenfrüchten enthalten" → ignore, allergen not kashrut.

The lesson: plant ≠ automatically cleaner. Read the list, not the category.
On the “made in Iceland” one: no non-dairy yogurt actually made in Iceland was found on Austrian shelves — the real one is Icelandic Provisions oat-milk skyr, a US product. Other plant options in Austria: Joya · SPAR Veggie · dmBio — same drill, check the tub. And skyr is out — see below.

Skyr: skip it 🔴

🔎 Researched Not from Gil — he said he didn't know what skyr was. This is sourced research — his view was asked for, not issued.

You asked whether skyr is OK everywhere. It isn't — and the reason isn't the milk or the rennet. It's the category: in Austria skyr is a fresh cheese.

Austria's food code files it under "Käse"
The Österreichisches Lebensmittelbuch B 32, §3.3.1.1.11 — inside Chapter 3 Käse — opens: "Skyr ist ein Frischkäse…" BILLA's own shelf listing calls its skyr "Frischkäse, mager."
Gevinas akum ignores the rennet
Rambam: the decree applies whether it was set with a permitted or a forbidden substance. So "microbial rennet" and a clean label don't rescue it — which is exactly your own rule on cheese.
Most Austrian brands declare rennet
mikrobielles Lab is on the label of Arla, BILLA own-brand, Milbona (Lidl) and REWE ja!
Rennet-free ones don't fix it 🟡
Ja! Natürlich Bio-Skyr (confirmed in writing: "enthält kein Lab") and SPAR Natur*pur Bio-Skyr declare no rennet — but the fresh-cheese category is the problem, not the rennet.
The label trap 🟡
Rennet counts as a processing aid under EU law and need not be declared. Seeing Lab proves it's there; not seeing it proves little.
What to buy instead 🟢
Plain Naturjoghurt (chalav stam) or a plant yogurt — same job, none of the argument.

Researched — the E471 question

Emulsifiers: stop reading the label for it

🔎 Researched Gil gave a one-line lean; the legal and halachic detail below is sourced research, not his answer.

Gil said "likely similar logic to gelatin… possibly more lenient since they may be plant based." Here's what actually decides it.

The label will never tell you 🔴
EU law does not require an additive's animal or vegetable origin to be declared. Reg 1169/2011 Annex VII Part C asks for only two things: the class ("Emulgator") and the name/E-number. Silence is the legal default, not an oversight. The German consumer authority says it plainly: "Hersteller sind leider nicht verpflichtet, die Herkunft der Speisefettsäuren zu nennen."
Two marks that DO resolve it 🟢
Both are things you must find, not infer: ① a V-Label vegan mark — audited, and explicitly covers additives, carriers, aromas, enzymes and processing aids; ② an explicit "pflanzlichen Ursprungs" statement on the pack.
Don't treat them as one class 🟡
Strong cases (assume plant): E422 glycerol — the one with a documented EU vegetable-only industry statement — plus E471 / E472. Weaker cases, where "probably vegetable" has no documentation behind it: E570, E572, E491–495, E430–436. Those are the ones worth putting back.
Animal fat isn't theoretical
EFSA's own re-evaluation names tallow and lard as permitted source materials for E471. Also honest: no sourced figure exists for the animal-vs-plant split — every "mostly plant-based" claim traces back to nothing.
The real halachic hinge מעמיד
The gelatin comparison stands or falls on davar hama'amid — and classically that means form-giving, not merely helpful. So the aisle question isn't the E-number, it's: would this product physically collapse without it? If yes → treat it like gelatin. If it only improves texture → that's the lenient side.
It's a live machlokes 🟡
Does a maintaining agent count as ma'amid? Minchat Yitzchak 6:71 says no; Rav Mordechai Eliyahu says yes — documented in Peninei Halakha 37:2. Also worth knowing: all six major agencies list this family as "requires certification," but that is an agency standard, not a ruling that the food is forbidden.
Bottom line for the aisle: don't scan for the origin — it isn't there. Look for the V-Label. Failing that, ask whether the emulsifier is holding the product together. Sources verified verbatim against Reg 1169/2011, EFSA, and the Hebrew text of Peninei Halakha.
