Section 1

Driving in Austria Verified 2026-05-16

Austria is left-hand-drive, right-side-of-road, the same as continental Europe. Roads are well-maintained, signage clear. The handful of things that surprise foreigners are below.

Vignette (motorway toll)

To drive on any Austrian autobahn (A-roads) or expressway (S-roads) you need a motorway vignette. Without one = fine of €120+ at any toll check.

Critical 10-day vignette: €12.80 (2026 price, raised from €11.50). Available as digital (linked to license plate) or physical sticker. Most rental cars come with the vignette already included — confirm at the Sixt counter on pickup so you don't double-pay. Source: ASFINAG official shop
Useful The vignette covers all autobahns/expressways EXCEPT a few special sections that have their own separate toll — notably the A10 Tauern Tunnel and the A9 Bosrucktunnel/Gleinalmtunnel. None of these are on your planned route (Salzburg → Hallstatt → Berchtesgaden → Hohe Tauern via Grossglockner).

Grossglockner High Alpine Road — separate toll

Critical — only if you drive it Day ticket: €46.50/car (summer 2026). Not covered by vignette — separate private road. Buy at the toll booth or online (advance online = €42.50). Reduced €36.50/car for entries after 18:00. Includes the entire 48 km route + all viewpoints. Source: grossglockner.at

Speed limits

  • City / built-up area: 50 km/h (≈31 mph)
  • Rural roads: 100 km/h (≈62 mph)
  • Autobahn: 130 km/h (≈81 mph) daytime
  • Some Alpine autobahns (A10, A12, A13, A14): 110 km/h overnight (22:00 - 05:00) to reduce noise. Watch for "IG-L" signs in Tirol/Styria — these enforce 100 km/h for air-quality reasons.

Source: oesterreich.gv.at official · Wikipedia summary

Lights

Daytime running lights are mandatory on all motorised vehicles. Modern rental cars do this automatically — but verify on pickup. Dipped headlights required in tunnels, rain, and reduced visibility.

Nice-to-know July = peak summer, no snow chains, no winter tyres needed. All-season tyres on the rental are fine.

Mountain road manners

  • "Bergauf hat Vorfahrt" — uphill traffic has right of way on narrow mountain roads. If you're going downhill and meet someone coming up, you reverse to the nearest pull-out.
  • Pull-outs (Ausweichstellen) are signed and frequent on alpine passes. Use them to let faster traffic through — locals know the road, you don't.
  • Engine braking on descents — drop to a lower gear instead of riding the brakes. Especially on Grossglockner switchbacks.
  • Gas stations are frequent on main routes; sparser in remote alpine valleys. Refuel before climbing a pass.
  • Roundabouts are common at town entries. Traffic already in the roundabout has priority. Signal on exit.

Drinking and driving

Critical Legal BAC limit: 0.05% (0.5 g/L) for licensed drivers. Lower than US 0.08 and stricter enforcement. Realistic interpretation: one beer with dinner if you're not driving for several hours; otherwise nothing. Source: oesterreich.gv.at
Section 2

Cross-border to Germany (Berchtesgaden) Verified 2026-05-16

Berchtesgaden, Königssee, the Eagle's Nest — all just over the German border, ~30 min from Salzburg. The good news: Schengen means no checkpoint typically. The slightly annoying news: vignettes don't transfer.

Nice-to-know Open border (Schengen) — usually no checkpoint, no stop. Germany has re-introduced occasional spot checks since 2024; if waved over, just show passport. Nothing to declare.
Useful — vignette doesn't transfer Austria's vignette does NOT cover Germany. Germany has no general motorway toll for passenger cars (the "PKW-Maut" was scrapped in 2019). You can drive Berchtesgaden roads freely with no German toll.
Critical — confirm at Sixt counter Cross-border allowed? Most rentals from Salzburg allow Germany (Sixt, Avis, Hertz, Europcar typically include EU+Schengen). VERIFY on pickup — get it in writing. Crossing without permission can void insurance.

German emergency numbers (in case)

  • 110 — Police (Polizei)
  • 112 — Fire / medical (works for everything, EU-wide)

If you only remember one number across both countries: 112 works everywhere.

Section 3

License + IDP Verified 2026-05-16

An Israeli driver's license is in Hebrew. To drive legally in Austria, you should carry an International Driving Permit (IDP / רישיון נהיגה בינלאומי) alongside it — a translation document, not a substitute.

Critical Bring BOTH: your Israeli license + IDP. Austria is one of the EU countries that strictly enforces the IDP requirement for non-EU licenses. Driving without one when stopped = fine (€100+). Rental agencies often ask too. Source: Schengen Traveler

How to get an IDP in Israel

  • Issued by MEMSI Touring (ממסי) — the official issuer in Israel.
  • Bring: valid Israeli license, passport-style photo, your passport. Apply in person at a MEMSI office (Tel Aviv, Jerusalem, Haifa branches).
  • Cost ~₪100-150, valid 1-3 years depending on type. Issued on the spot.
  • Do it at least 1-2 weeks before the trip — not the day of.

Source: memsi.co.il official — verify current fee + branches on the site.

Section 4

Currency + payments Verified 2026-05-16

Austria uses the Euro (€). The unfamiliar thing is how card-heavy Austria is — almost everything takes Visa/Mastercard. Different mental model than cash-first destinations.

Nice-to-know — cards work everywhere Visa + Mastercard accepted in 95%+ of cafés, restaurants, supermarkets, gas stations, apartments. Apple Pay and Google Pay work at most contactless terminals (same hardware as US/UK).

ATMs (Bankomat)

  • Widely available in any town. Look for Bankomat signs (any major Austrian bank: Erste, Bank Austria, Raiffeisen, BAWAG).
  • Use bank-branded ATMs, not the standalone "Euronet" or "Travelex" ones — those charge €3-7 per withdrawal + bad exchange rate.
  • Always choose to be charged in EUR, not your home currency — declining "dynamic currency conversion" saves you 3-5%.

Tipping

  • Restaurants: round up to the next round number, or add ~5-10% for good service. No fixed expectation. Say the total you want to pay when handing over the card ("zwanzig" = €20 if your bill is €18.40).
  • Taxi: round up €1-2.
  • Hotel housekeeping / luggage: €1-2 per service, optional.
  • Cafés / quick service: round up the change.

Cash floor

Carry €100-200 in cash for: parking meters (some still coin-only), mountain hut snacks, small village shops, tips, and emergency. Beyond that, cards.

Section 5

Connectivity Verified 2026-05-16

Israeli mobile plans don't automatically include EU roaming the way EU plans do across the bloc. Check your carrier before assuming your SIM "just works."

Useful — check before flying Call your carrier (Cellcom / Pelephone / Partner / HOT / Golan): ask about Austria roaming and what it costs. Most have a daily flat-rate package (~₪30/day for unlimited data) — cheaper than per-MB.

Alternative: Austrian SIM

  • A1, Magenta, Drei (Three) — three main Austrian carriers. Prepaid SIMs sold at Salzburg Airport arrivals hall + every Spar/Billa.
  • Typical tourist deal: ~€10-20 for a week of unlimited data + some calls.
  • HoT (Hofer Telekom) sold at Hofer (Aldi) supermarkets is often the cheapest. €15 = 4 weeks unlimited.
  • Bring your passport — registration required.

eSIM (easiest option)

If your phone supports eSIM (iPhone XS+, most modern Android), Airalo / Holafly / Saily sell Austria or EU eSIMs you can activate before you even land. ~$5-15 for a week of data. No physical SIM swap.

WiFi + offline maps

  • Apartments + cafés have WiFi nearly universally. Apartment WiFi password = in check-in info.
  • Download offline Google Maps tiles BEFORE you leave — at home on unlimited home WiFi. Tap your profile in Google Maps → Offline Maps → Select Your Own Map → choose Salzburg + Salzkammergut + Berchtesgaden + Hohe Tauern. Saves you mobile data + works in dead zones (Grossglockner has patchy coverage).

Emergency + safety Verified 2026-05-16

Critical — memorise one number 112 — EU-wide emergency. Works in Austria + Germany + everywhere in the EU. English-speaking operators. They route to police, ambulance, or fire as needed.

Austria-specific numbers

Police (Polizei) 133
Ambulance (Rettung) 144
Fire (Feuerwehr) 122
Mountain rescue (Bergrettung) 140
EU-wide emergency 112
Roadside breakdown (ÖAMTC) 120

Hospitals in your area

  • Salzburg — LKH Salzburg (Landeskrankenhaus / Uniklinikum): main regional hospital, full A&E. Müllner Hauptstraße 48. ~10 min from Linzergasse Chabad.
  • Salzkammergut (Gosau base): nearest clinic to Gosau/Hallstatt is Bad Ischl Salzkammergut-Klinikum (~30 min from Gosau).
  • Berchtesgaden (Germany): Kreisklinik Berchtesgadener Land — covers the German side of any hiking. ~20 min from Berchtesgaden centre.
  • Hohe Tauern area: Tauernklinikum Zell am See — main hospital for the Grossglockner / Kaprun / Zell am See area.

Pharmacy (Apotheke)

  • Daytime: any town has at least one Apotheke. Look for the green-cross sign.
  • Night + Sunday — "Notdienst" rotating: in each district, one pharmacy stays open 24/7 on rotation. The sign on EVERY pharmacy door lists which one is on tonight + that pharmacy's address.
  • Search "Apothekensuche" + town name online for the current Notdienst-Apotheke.

Travel insurance

Useful — verify before flying Make sure your travel insurance includes medical evacuation (helicopter rescue in the Alps is expensive — €5000+ without coverage). Israeli health funds (Clalit / Maccabi / Meuhedet / Leumit) usually offer add-on travel coverage; verify Austria + Germany + mountain activities are included. Keep the policy PDF on your phone and a printed copy in your bag.
Section 7

Cultural + etiquette quick notes

Sundays

Almost everything is closed Sundays under the federal Ladenschlussgesetz. Move day Jul 26 is the one Sunday on your trip — plan grocery stocking around it. Spar at the Hauptbahnhof (Salzburg main station) is one of the few options open on a Sunday.

Meal times

  • Breakfast (Frühstück): 07:00 - 10:00
  • Lunch (Mittagessen): 12:00 - 14:00 typical, hot food window
  • Dinner (Abendessen): 18:00 - 21:00. Many kitchens close at 21:00 — don't assume late dining like Spain or Israel.
  • Quiet hours (Ruhezeit): after 22:00 in most municipalities. Apartment neighbours expect noise reduction — close windows, lower music, etc.

Greetings

  • Entering any shop: "Grüß Gott" (literally "greet God" — standard Austrian/Bavarian, not religious) or "Guten Tag." Not greeting is mildly rude.
  • Leaving: "Auf Wiedersehen" (formal) or "Tschüss" (casual). Or just "Danke, schönen Tag!" ("thanks, nice day").
  • Thank you: "Danke" — and "bitte" means both "please" and "you're welcome."

Other small things

  • Tap water is excellent and free everywhere — including alpine fountains. Ask for "Leitungswasser" at a restaurant if you want free tap (some places charge for bottled).
  • Cyclists have right of way on marked bike paths — they appear next to sidewalks painted red. Don't walk on them.
  • Smoking is banned in restaurants + bars (since 2019). Outside seating OK.

  • Check-in window: 14:00 - 16:00 typical. Check-out: 10:00 - 11:00. Always written in your booking confirmation.
  • WhatsApp the host 24-48h before arrival to confirm key collection method. Self check-in (lockbox + code) is common; in-person handover also common.
  • Salzburg Friday — Shabbat key prep: arrange Friday-afternoon key drop BEFORE candle-lighting (20:35). Once Shabbat starts you can't carry keys outside (no eruv in Salzburg).
  • Take photos on arrival — pre-existing damage, scratches, marks. Insurance against false damage claims at checkout.
  • Verify on arrival: hot water working, induction plate working (matters for the kitchenette), WiFi password works, all doors lock.
  • Garbage rules — apartments often have strict sorting (paper / glass / plastic / organic / residual). Check the kitchen instructions; fines exist but rare for short-term guests.
Section 10

Money + budget tips

  • Trip total budget ≈ ₪13,000 — see Costs page for the full breakdown.
  • Cash float: €100-200 in mixed denominations. Bring €20s + €50s; avoid €200/€500 (some shops refuse).
  • Use a card with no foreign transaction fee — Israeli banks: Cal Choice / Max It / Amex Israel "טיולים" are common no-FX choices. Verify with your bank; standard cards add 2.5-3.5% per transaction.
  • Notify your bank before flying so they don't lock the card on first Austria swipe. Most Israeli banks have an in-app "travel notice" toggle.
  • Carry a backup card separately from your main wallet (in the apartment, in luggage). If main card is lost/cloned, you're not stranded.
  • Parking meters: many are now app-based (HANDY Parken / EasyPark). Some small towns still coin-only — that's why you carry €1/€2 coins.

Austria-specific additions

  • Fleece / midlayer — Eisriesenwelt ice cave is below freezing year-round; Schafberg summit is ~12°C even at sunset in July.

  • Hiking shoes — not boots, but real shoes. Crocs for the apartment and the lake.
  • Swimsuit + quick-dry towel — Wolfgangsee swim, possible Lake Hallstatt dip.
  • Sun hat + sunscreen — boats on the Königssee are open-top.
  • Power adapter — Austria uses Type F (Schuko, same as continental EU).
  • EU SIM or eSIM — Austria has good mobile coverage; useful for cave-ticket booking on the road.
  • Refillable water bottle — Allison's Owala. Tap water is clean.
  • Cash €100-200 — Austria is card-friendly but some mountain huts and small cog-railway snacks are cash-only.
  • Headlamp / small flashlight — for the ice cave if your guide's carbide lamp falters.

🌿 Kosher finds — what we know as of May 2026

Tight constraint: there are no kosher restaurants in Salzburg, Hallstatt, or anywhere in the Salzkammergut. Closest is Vienna (~3hrs east). So:

  • Brewed coffee (espresso, drip, V60) from a recognized cafe is widely considered permissible per most kosher authorities — straight coffee + hot water doesn't need supervision (some opinions stricter; check with your rabbi if it matters to you). That means espresso + flat white = yes at any of the cafés below.
  • Milk — most cafés use commercial Austrian dairy. If you're chalav yisroel-strict, bring your own oat milk packets from home and ask politely; many Austrian third-wave cafés stock oat milk and will use it.
  • Mann's Bakery (Linzergasse 22, Salzburg) — the Chabad-adjacent bakery sells Shabbat challah + some pastries. Listed for completeness — this is bakery, not café.
  • Sealed-hechsher products at Spar — see packing list. Buy and bring to the café (or eat in advance and just order coffee).
  • No vegan/hechsher café in Salzburg currently confirmed as of May 2026. If we surface one during the rest of pre-trip planning, it'll appear here in bold.
